Learning Hub: For the Team

A team reference for understanding what the Learning Hub is, who it serves, how access and records work, and how the platform can grow without losing trust.

1. What Is the Learning Hub?

LACE is MLRI's learning initiative: a shared training platform for the Massachusetts legal aid community, built and run by the Massachusetts Law Reform Institute (MLRI). Its purpose is to strengthen legal aid by strengthening learning.

The Learning Hub is the experience we are building around Brightspace. It gives learners a cleaner front door for finding training, while Brightspace remains the system underneath for sign-in, course hosting, enrollments, completion records, and certificates.

Brightspace (D2L): the LMS under the Learning Hub. Where Brightspace terms appear below, they are the actual product names, not simplified stand-ins.

How does it support the Training Unit?

The Learning Hub helps the Training Unit move from one-off training delivery to a more reliable learning system. It gives the team a shared catalog, structured user profiles, clearer access rules, durable records, and better signals about what learners need next.

How is the Learning Hub different from Brightspace?

LayerWhat happens thereRole in the system
Learning HubLanding page, dashboard, search, filters, recommendations, profile updates, and the clean course entry point.The front door and learner experience.
BrightspaceAuthentication, course hosting, enrollments, progress, completion, certificates, grades, and official audit records.The LMS engine and source of truth.
Supabase / custom layerFast catalog data, engagement signals, profile preferences, ratings, search behavior, and dashboard support.The support layer that lets us build features Brightspace alone does not give us cleanly.
Source of truth: Brightspace is the official record for enrollments, progress, completion, certificates, and grades. The Learning Hub can display and enrich that information, but it does not replace Brightspace as the record keeper.

How the pieces fit together

Three layers, each with one job. Learners spend their time in the Learning Hub, Brightspace keeps the official records underneath, and Supabase holds the extra data that powers search and dashboards.

The front door Learning Hub Search, dashboard, resume, and certificates: the experience learners actually see and use.
→
The source of truth Brightspace Sign-in, enrollments, completion, and certificates: the official records that count.
→
The support layer Supabase Catalog, search, feedback, and dashboard data: the extra layer we control.

Learner flow

From first sign-in to a saved certificate, each part does one job and hands off to the next.

Step 1 You sign in Brightspace confirms who you are through a trusted login.
→
Step 2 The Learning Hub reads your profile Your user type, jurisdiction, and practice areas shape what appears.
→
Step 3 You find a course The dashboard, search, filters, and recommendations point you to relevant training.
→
Step 4 You finish, it is recorded A real completion action is stored in Brightspace and can produce a certificate.

Is it live yet, and what works today?

It is an early working version, not the finished product. Today the course wrapper can deliver courses, the Learning Hub connects to Brightspace through a secure login (OAuth), and it can read a test course and sync that course's basic information into our own database. Learner dashboards, manager views, and reporting are still being built.

2. Who Uses It?

Every learner has a profile: professional role, jurisdiction, practice area, and the access level that fits them. That profile drives what they see.

User types

User typeWho they areWhat they can reachWhy this tier exists
Licensed attorneyA bar-licensed lawyer.Broadest access, including attorney-only practice material.Carries full professional responsibility for legal work.
ParalegalWorks under attorney supervision.Skills and substantive training that fits supervised work.Can do advanced work, but under a lawyer's direction.
AdvocateA trained non-lawyer who helps clients.Advocate-safe training within a defined scope.Needs useful training without crossing into lawyer-only authority.
Community memberGeneral public or least-verified user.Foundational, public-facing material only.Highest risk of overstepping, so the tightest gate.

User type vs. system role

A user type describes the learner's professional identity. A system role describes what the person can do inside the software.

ConceptExamplesWhat it controls
User typeAttorney, paralegal, advocate, community member.Course access, legal-risk boundaries, and certificate wording.
System roleLearner, manager, admin, org administrator.Software permissions, such as whether someone can view team progress or manage settings.
Org Administrator: a separate behind-the-scenes role with broad control of the system. It runs the platform; it is not a learner tier.

Profile attributes

User attributes are controlled profile labels that travel with a learner. They let the platform sort people into the right training without manual course-by-course placement.

AttributeWhat it recordsExample
User typeThe person's professional tier.Attorney, paralegal, advocate, community member.
JurisdictionWhich state or states they work in.Massachusetts; later New York, Maine, or more than one state.
Practice areaThe kind of law or service area they work in.Housing, benefits, family, immigration.
Bar numberAn attorney's license number.For attorney certificate and compliance workflows.
Onboarding dateWhen the learner joined the Learning Hub.Used for welcome flows and new-user reporting.

Onboarding and profile collection

Onboarding captures enough about a learner to show the right training from day one.

AudienceHow they are addedWhat we need
MLRI staffMostly automatic, using known HR or staff records and bulk attribute uploads.Name, email, user type, jurisdiction, practice area, organization, and onboarding date.
Partners and volunteersThrough a Learning Hub sign-up flow, invitation, or admin-created account.Basic identity, organization, role, jurisdiction, practice area, and any required acknowledgements.
Future external usersThrough a more formal front-door workflow with eligibility checks where needed.Only the fields needed to route training, confirm eligibility, and support certificates.
Collect less by default: avoid sensitive client information, unnecessary demographic detail, free-text legal facts, or anything we do not need for access, training, reporting, compliance, or support.

How profile information changes over time

  • Learners should update low-risk preferences, such as practice areas or interests, through a future My Profile page.
  • Admins should control high-impact fields, such as user type, organization, jurisdiction, and eligibility when those fields affect access or certificates.
  • Course activities can collect one-time confirmations, such as a UPL acknowledgement, bar-number prompt, or welcome survey response.

3. How Does Access Work?

Access depends on the learner's user type and their role in the software. People reach the training they need, and never content or credentials beyond their role.

How the platform decides what someone can see

SignalWhat it controlsExample
User typeProfessional access level and certificate language.Attorney-only material stays limited to licensed attorneys.
JurisdictionWhich state's content appears.A Massachusetts learner sees Massachusetts-specific law first.
Practice areaWhich topic areas are recommended or assigned.A housing advocate sees housing training prominently.
Organization / groupTeam views, required trainings, and manager reporting.A manager sees assigned team progress, not the whole system.

What if someone has more than one role?

When someone has more than one jurisdiction, practice area, or organization, they can belong to more than one group. Access should be additive for appropriate training, but conservative for legal-risk boundaries.

The bright line: a non-lawyer must never receive access, certificates, or completion wording that suggests authority to practice law. When there is uncertainty, the safer user type wins until an admin verifies the record.

Who can view learner progress?

ViewerWhat they should see
LearnerTheir own courses, progress, completions, and certificates.
Manager / supervisorProgress for learners they are responsible for, especially required or overdue training.
Training UnitCompletion and engagement patterns needed to support courses and improve training.
Admins / OpsSystem health, sync issues, account status, and audit-support information.
Individual vs. summary views: managers may need individual completion status for required training. Broader evaluation reporting should prefer summaries unless individual-level detail is necessary.

Login, security, and offboarding

  • Login: learners enter through a trusted Brightspace-supported sign-in path, using OAuth or another approved authentication setup.
  • Approved users: account creation, invitations, or eligibility checks decide who can enter the platform.
  • Leaving an organization: the account should be deactivated or removed from the organization's active groups, not deleted.
  • Forgotten passwords: the reset process should follow the configured Brightspace or identity-provider flow, not a separate Learning Hub-only workaround.

Certificates and training records

Certificates are built from Brightspace completion records and must match the learner's verified user type. A paralegal, advocate, or community member should never receive attorney credential language.

Certificate fieldWhat it should include
Learner nameThe name tied to the Brightspace account.
Course titleThe completed course or course version.
Completion dateThe date Brightspace records the course as complete.
Role-appropriate languageAttorney/CLE language only when the learner is verified as an attorney.
Bar numberOnly when needed for attorney compliance or certificate rules.
Certificate sharing: sharing certificates on LinkedIn or other platforms should be a future product and policy decision, not a default assumption. We should decide what public wording is appropriate before enabling it.
CLE credit: the system is built to support Continuing Legal Education. Brightspace records official visits and completions that can feed jurisdictional CLE reporting, and CLE language appears only for a verified attorney. Whether a specific course is accredited for CLE credit is a per-course decision that is not yet established.
Protecting learner information: we collect less by default and store only what we need. Brightspace holds the official records, the custom layer holds limited engagement and profile data, and sensitive client information is never collected.

4. How Do Courses Work?

A Learning Hub course is built by us, wrapped in the Learning Hub experience, and hosted in Brightspace. The learner sees a clean, focused course; Brightspace records the official activity underneath.

Who builds courses?

One central course owner builds courses as custom HTML files, tests them, then uploads them to Brightspace. Custom HTML is the default course model; native Brightspace tools such as quizzes, surveys, or discussions are used only when they are simpler than custom HTML for a specific need.

As LACE expands to more states, a course is built as one "course family" (for example, Eviction Defense) with a separate offering for each state, because law, forms, deadlines, and local resources differ. Shared material is reused, but learner-facing legal guidance stays specific to each state.

Brightspace structure

LevelWhat it means

Training areaA broad category, such as Legal Skills or Substantive Law.

SectionAn optional split inside an offering for a group, cohort, or scheduled run.

Course terms

TermPlain meaning

QuizA Brightspace or custom knowledge check that can help verify completion.

Legal Skills vs. Substantive Law

  • Legal Skills covers broad professional workflows: writing, research, ethics, interviewing, case planning, and other skills useful across practice areas.
  • Substantive Law covers knowledge tied to a specific legal area: housing, benefits, family law, immigration, and similar topics.

What completion means

Opening a page is not enough. Completion should require at least one real action: a quiz, check-in, reflection, scenario, upload, acknowledgement, or deliberate mark-complete.

Completion can mean different things for different courses, but it should always be documented clearly in Brightspace so the record and certificate mean something.

The clean course view and wrapper

Learning Hub courses can open in a focused view that hides the standard LMS menus. The wrapper gives us consistent navigation and visual design, while Brightspace still records visits, progress, and completion.

Wrapper tracking rule: course navigation should point to Brightspace's official topic URLs. That keeps topic visits and completion rules connected to the LMS instead of bypassing it.

Where content lives

PlaceWhat belongs there

Brightspace Organization FilesShared styling, logos, templates, and institutional assets used across courses.

Manage Files cleanup rule: if a file is deleted from a course's Manage Files, also remove the matching content link. Otherwise learners may hit broken links or see progress numbers that do not make sense.

5. How Are Records, Data, and Reports Handled?

There are two kinds of data: the official training record in Brightspace, and engagement data in the custom layer. They work together but are not the same thing.

Official records vs. engagement data

SourceWhat it tracksHow to treat it
BrightspaceEnrollments, course starts, topic visits, progress, completions, grades, and certificates.Official source of truth for audit and compliance records.
Learning Hub / SupabaseSearch terms, filters, ratings, profile preferences, drop-off points, and dashboard interactions.Helpful operating and improvement data, not the official completion record.

What activity data is reliable?

  • Reliable for records: enrollment, completion, certificate issue, grades, required activities, and Brightspace-tracked visits.
  • Useful but interpret carefully: time on page, video watch behavior, search terms, ratings, and drop-off points. These show patterns, not full explanations.
  • Worth combining: completion data plus feedback gives a better picture than either one alone.
Topic visit: Brightspace's record that a learner opened a specific module or page in a course. These visits can feed progress tracking and completion rules.

What reports will we need?

AudienceLikely reporting need
Training UnitEnrollment, completion, feedback, course performance, learner demand, and where people get stuck.
ManagersTeam progress, overdue required training, certificates, and upcoming deadlines.
PartnersParticipation and completion for their own learners or agreed program groups.
Funders / grantsAggregate participation, completion, reach, topic coverage, and evaluation outcomes.
Internal evaluationUsefulness, learner confidence, course quality, equity of access, and training gaps.
Admins / ITSync status, account issues, broken links, access errors, and system health.

How do we collect feedback?

Feedback can come from course ratings, short surveys, reflection prompts, support requests, search behavior, and completion patterns. The key is to use it as an improvement loop: what learners look for, where they struggle, and what training the team should build or revise next.

Key technical terms

TermPlain meaning
LMS (Learning Management System)The software that hosts courses, signs learners in, tracks progress, and stores records. Brightspace is our LMS.
Brightspace (D2L)Our LMS, made by a company called D2L. It is the engine underneath the Learning Hub.
OAuth 2.0A standard way to sign in through a trusted provider without handing your password to every app you use.
SSO (single sign-on)One login that carries across the Learning Hub and Brightspace, so learners do not have to sign in twice. Still being set up.
APIA controlled doorway between systems. Only specific, permitted information passes through, and only for approved requests.

SupabaseThe database service behind the custom layer. It supports speed, search, dashboards, and engagement features; it does not replace Brightspace records.
Custom frontendThe Learning Hub interface we control, layered over Brightspace so the learner experience can be cleaner and more useful.

6. How Does the Learning Hub Grow and Stay Current?

The Learning Hub grows by configuration: adding learners, states, courses, or practice areas should not require rebuilding it.

Growth model

  • More learners: add users through invitations, bulk upload, HR-backed onboarding, or external sign-up flows.
  • More courses: add course offerings under a predictable Brightspace structure.
  • More practice areas: add controlled attributes and catalog filters, then use them for recommendations and enrollment.
  • More states: add new jurisdiction branches alongside Massachusetts instead of mixing state-specific law together.

Jurisdiction-first expansion

Massachusetts starts as its own branch. New York, Maine, or another state can be added later as separate branches. A learner licensed or working in more than one state can carry more than one jurisdiction and be placed into the right state-specific training.

Minor vs. major law updates

Kind of changeWhat countsWhat we do
Minor updateTypos, broken links, wording cleanup, examples, or small clarifications.Edit the live offering and document the change lightly.
Major updateA meaningful law, regulation, procedure, or practice change.Create a new course offering or version, then decide who needs notice, review, or retake.
Who decides? the Training Unit should own the content decision, with legal subject-matter input when needed. The platform should support the decision; it should not silently decide legal significance on its own.

Old versions and outdated materials

We follow an archive, never delete rule. Old course versions should be renamed clearly, hidden from the public catalog, and preserved so past completions and certificates still make sense.

  • Archive old Brightspace offerings instead of deleting them.
  • Hide outdated Learning Hub cards so learners do not start stale content by mistake.
  • Keep records attached to the old version so a past certificate can still be audited.
  • Document major changes enough that staff can explain what changed and why.

Learner notifications and past completions

SituationWhat learners needWhat happens to past completion
Minor correctionNo notification unless the fix affects learner action.Past completion stands.
Important legal updateEmail, dashboard banner, or assigned update review.Past completion usually stands, but learners may need to review the update.
Critical changeRequired retake or required update module with a due date.Past completion remains historically true; current compliance may require the new version.

Manager Dashboard and Learning Groups

Both are useful Brightspace features, but they are not required on day one.

FeatureWhat it doesHow to treat it
Learning GroupsAutomatically enrolls learners based on attributes like user type, jurisdiction, practice area, or organization.Useful once attributes are stable. Learners can belong to more than one group.
Manager DashboardLets supervisors view team progress in one place.Useful for required training and deadlines. Optional for a small pilot.

7. What Is the Learner Experience?

The learner path is direct: sign in, find relevant training, start or resume a course, finish it, and find the certificate later.

Mobile use

The Learning Hub works in a phone browser and can be installed like an app by choosing "Add to Home Screen." After that, it opens from the home screen with its own icon and a cleaner full-screen feel.

Installable app (PWA): the Learning Hub is a Progressive Web App, so there is nothing to download from an app store and nothing separate for learners to update.

Are there any mobile PWA limitations right now?

Yes, a small one. On iOS, an installed Progressive Web App can feel very app-like while the learner stays inside the Learning Hub. When a learner opens a course page that still lives directly on Brightspace, iOS may show the Safari browser interface because Brightspace is a separate website outside the Learning Hub app scope.

Current approach: the Learning Hub is still mobile-friendly, but a few course launches may briefly feel less integrated on iPhone or iPad while they open Brightspace-hosted pages.
Plan to address it: move toward Learning Hub-owned course URLs such as /learn/[course-or-module]. Brightspace remains the source of truth for enrollment, progress, completion, and certificates, while more of the learner-facing reading and course navigation renders inside the Learning Hub itself. The Welcome course and Eviction course can keep Brightspace URLs during the transition.

Finding courses

  • Search: learners should be able to look for courses by topic, keyword, or need.
  • Browse: learners should be able to scan by category, such as Legal Skills or Substantive Law.
  • Filters: filters can narrow by jurisdiction, practice area, role, format, or completion status.
  • Recommendations: practice-area recommendations are a good future layer once profile data is reliable.

Progress and certificates

Learner questionWhere the answer should appear
What have I started?A dashboard area for in-progress courses and resume links.
What have I completed?A completed courses or history view, based on Brightspace records.
Where are my certificates?A certificates area or course-completion view that points back to the official Brightspace certificate.
What should I take next?Recommended or required courses based on profile attributes and Training Unit priorities.

Support paths

IssueLikely owner
Course content questionTraining Unit or course owner.
Certificate or completion concernTraining Unit first, with Brightspace admin help if the record needs review.
Login or password issueBrightspace support, IT, or the identity provider path configured for the site.
Broken link, display problem, or dashboard issueLearning Hub admin / technical support.

Accessibility

We are building toward accessible courses: video and audio go in the Media Library with captions and transcripts, and accessibility is a current hardening priority. The formal target standard, such as an ADA, Section 508, or WCAG conformance level, is still being set.

Languages

The Brightspace interface can show some text in multiple languages based on locale settings, including English, Spanish, Hmong, Somali, and Karen. Course content itself is authored per language and is mostly English for the pilot. Which courses get translated, and into which languages, is a future decision.
