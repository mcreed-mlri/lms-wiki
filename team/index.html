What is this?

People

Who are the user types?

LACE sorts every user into one of four tiers. Access rises with professional responsibility — and tightens where the risk of overstepping the law rises.

User typeWho they areWhat they can reachWhy this tier
Licensed attorneyA bar-licensed lawyer.Broadest access, including attorney-only practice material.Carries full professional responsibility for the work.
ParalegalWorks under attorney supervision.Skills and substantive training that fits supervised work.Capable and trained, but acting under a lawyer's direction.
AdvocateA trained non-lawyer who helps clients.Advocate-safe training within a defined scope.Valuable front-line help, kept clear of lawyer-only territory.
Community memberGeneral public; the least-verified user.Foundational, public-facing material only.Highest risk of overstepping the law, so the tightest gate.
Org Administrator: a separate, behind-the-scenes role with full control of the system. It is held by very few people and used sparingly — it runs the platform, it is not a learner tier.
The bright line: a non-lawyer must never receive access, certificates, or completion language that suggests authority to practice law. Honoring this line is the main reason the tiers exist, and it is enforced for every non-lawyer user.
Why this matters: the tiers do two jobs at once — they decide what material a person can reach, and they decide the certificate and completion wording a person receives. So no one is met with content, or credentials, beyond their role.

What are user attributes?

User attributes are small profile labels — like badges — that travel with each learner. They tell the platform who someone is, so the right courses reach them without anyone placing them by hand.

AttributeWhat it recordsExample
User typeThe person's tier.Attorney, paralegal, advocate, community member
JurisdictionWhich state(s) they work in.Massachusetts (can be more than one)
Practice areaThe kind of law they work in.Housing, benefits, family, immigration
Bar numberAn attorney's license number.For attorneys only
Onboarding dateWhen they joined LACE.A date
  • The labels do the sorting. When a learner's attributes match a course's intended audience, the platform can enroll them automatically — no sign-up form, no administrator placing them by hand.
  • People can belong to more than one group. A learner can carry several jurisdictions or practice areas at once (for example, Housing and Benefits), so they receive everything relevant to them.
  • The labels stay consistent everywhere. The same attributes drive enrollment, access, and reporting, so nothing has to be re-entered course by course.
Attribute: a controlled label stored on an account — a badge the platform reads, not free-text notes. The platform supports up to 50 such labels, and a person can hold more than one value where it makes sense.
Why this matters: attorneys and advocates land in the correct state-specific and subject-matter material on their own, without waiting on an administrator. Less manual work means fewer gaps and fewer mistakes.
Access & Safety

Who can see what?

Every user only finds and opens the material meant for their tier. Here is how that decision is made — and why restricted material never reaches the wrong screen.

How the platform decides

  1. Sign in: the platform confirms who you are.
  2. Look up your profile: it loads your user type and status.
  3. Check the rules: it compares your type against each item's intended audience.
  4. Build your view: it assembles only the material you are allowed to find.
  5. Show results: your screen receives the already-filtered list — nothing more.
  6. Open a course: Brightspace checks one more time at the door before letting you in.
Discovery vs. access: discovery is searching or browsing the catalog; access is actually opening a course. LACE controls both, with the same rules.

Who sees whose progress

  • Learners see their own progress only. They cannot view other people's records or any admin tools.
  • Supervisors see their team. A manager view shows progress for the learners assigned to them — useful for tracking required training.
  • Two supervisors can follow one learner. Many legal-aid staff answer to both a local supervisor and a program director, so a learner can be linked to two managers and both get real-time visibility.
Secondary Manager: Brightspace normally allows a learner just one manager. We added a second manager link so a supervisor and a director can both see a person's progress at the same time.
Why this matters: the platform decides what you may see before anything reaches your screen. Restricted material is never sent and then hidden — it simply never arrives. A community member cannot stumble onto attorney-only content, even by searching for it.

How is access kept secure?

Several safeguards work together so the right people reach the right material and records stay trustworthy. Each one is a protection — the technology just delivers it.

SafeguardWhat it doesYour reassurance
Trusted sign-in (OAuth 2.0)Identity is confirmed through Brightspace's standard sign-in.Passwords aren't shared around; only confirmed users get in.
Controlled user attributesAccess is driven by a fixed user type on each account.Access follows a verified label, never a guess or a typed-in title.
One system of recordBrightspace is the single home for enrollments, completions, and certificates.Records can't drift between systems — there is one source of truth.
Role-based viewsEach person sees only their own view: learner, manager, or admin.A learner can't reach admin tools or another person's data.
Server-side filteringThe access rules run on our servers, not in the web browser.Restricted material is never sent to the screen in the first place.

Where data is stored

WhereWhat it holdsWhy there
BrightspaceOfficial records: personal information, enrollments, completions, and certificates.The single, secure system of record.
Custom data layerA fast working copy of course info, plus searches, feedback, and engagement.Keeps the platform quick and responsive.
How the two connect: the custom layer reaches Brightspace only through an authorized doorway (OAuth 2.0), so the platform sees only the data it is explicitly permitted to — and sensitive personal records stay in Brightspace, not copied elsewhere.

The terms, in plain words

OAuth 2.0: the industry-standard way to sign in through a trusted provider without handing your password to every app you use.
API: a controlled doorway between two systems. Only specific, permitted information passes through, and only for approved requests.
System of record: the single authoritative source for a piece of data. For enrollments and completions, that source is Brightspace.
Why this matters: no single safeguard carries the whole weight. Trusted sign-in confirms who you are, controlled attributes decide what you may reach, filtering keeps restricted material off the screen, and Brightspace keeps the official record. Together they mean appropriate access is the default, not something to police after the fact.
The Platform

How is Brightspace organized?

Brightspace arranges courses in a tree, from the whole site down to a single class. "Organization" here means the course structure — not MLRI's staff structure.

LevelWhat it is
MLRIThe top of our site.
MassachusettsThe state layer — with room to add more states later.
Training AreaA broad category: Legal Skills or Substantive Law.
ProgramA topic cluster, such as Housing Law or Intro to Legal Skills.
Course TemplateThe master container holding every version of a course. No one enrolls here.
Course OfferingThe actual course people take. Learners enroll here.
SectionAn optional split within an offering, for a specific group or scheduled run.
Template vs. Offering — the one to remember: a template is the master file cabinet for a course; an offering is the live class people actually take. Learners always enroll in an offering, never a template. That separation is what lets us retire an old version while keeping its records intact.
Why this matters: a clear, predictable structure makes courses easy to find, easy to expand to new states, and easy to update — without breaking the history of who completed what.

How does a course work?

A LACE course is built by us and hosted on Brightspace. Here's what one is made of, what it takes to "complete" it, and how learners are kept current when the law moves.

What a course is made of

LayerWhat it isWho owns it
Course contentThe pages, activities, and visuals a learner moves through.LACE builds it.
LACE wrapperThe consistent LACE look and navigation around that content.LACE builds it.
BrightspaceHosts the course, signs people in, tracks progress, keeps records.Brightspace admins.
Optional Brightspace toolsQuizzes, discussions, and the like — used only when simpler than building it ourselves.Case-by-case.
Clean view: LACE courses open in a focused view that hides the standard LMS menus, so a learner sees only the legal material — not a cluttered software screen. It respects busy people's time.

What "completion" means

Opening or watching is not completion. A course counts as complete only after at least one real action — a quiz, a check-in, a reflection, or a deliberate mark-complete.
How it's tracked: Brightspace records that verifying action against the learner's account. That record is the official proof of completion — the same record any certificate is built from.

When the law changes

What changedDo learners hear about it?What we tell them
A minor edit (wording, typo)NoNothing — their record is unchanged.
The law or a regulation changedYesWhat changed, and whether they need to review.
A major rebuild of a courseMaybeOnly if they should review or retake it.
An old version retiredMaybeOnly if they must move to the new version.
Minor vs. major change: a small fix is edited in place. A major law change becomes a new version of the course, and the old one is archived — never deleted — so past completion records are always preserved. When a retake is required, affected learners can be notified automatically.
Why this matters: completion reflects genuine engagement, not just a page load — so records and certificates mean something. And no one is left quietly sitting on outdated law: meaningful changes come with a clear notice, while small edits don't create noise.
Optional & Data

Manager Dashboard & Learning Groups — do we need them?

Both are available Brightspace features. They are genuinely useful, but optional — especially at the start. Nothing breaks if we wait to turn them on.

FeatureWhat it doesDo we need it now?
Learning GroupsAutomatically enroll people into courses based on their attributes.Helpful once attributes are set. Optional at first — people can be enrolled by hand.
Manager DashboardLets a supervisor see their team's progress in one place.Useful if supervisors must track required training. Optional for a small pilot.
Think of these as switches: they can be turned on whenever we're ready. They are conveniences that save manual work — not prerequisites for launching.
Why this matters: we have powerful automation available, and we adopt it deliberately — only when it clearly saves real work. There's no pressure to use every feature on day one.
